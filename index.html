TERMINAL LIVE

In October, I was accepted into the hackathon TERMINAL Live, which involved programming an algorithm for a tower defense-style game. Along with two other people (though I was the one who thought up the strategy and programmed it in), we created an algorithm called Meteor (so called because it destroyed the strongest sample algorithm, Raptor), got to the semifinals through a bit of luck, and collectively won $2K! Our semifinal video is on the right, where we somehow defeated our losing matchup against ping cannon-type algorithms and got called out for having the strangest strategy known to man. Winning something here was pretty exhilarating and helped me overcome a bit of the imposter syndrome feeling by showing me that even though I didn’t know a whole lot of technical code, I could still do great things.

DubHacks

This year (my freshman year!) I was lucky enough to be accepted into and attend DubHacks, my first “traditional” hackathon, where I teamed up with three other people to create a technology-infused product in just 24 hours. Together, with me as the leader and sole website developer, we created DreamPlan (defunct because the api key expired) , a web application that helped people find UW classes tailored to their interests. Just type in some keywords or answer a questionnaire, and it intelligently searches the UW course catalog using Microsoft Azure technology to find the classes that are right for you—a great improvement over the basic MyUW search tool. Throughout the DubHacks experience, I learned quite a bit about teamwork and hackathons. DubHacks hammered into me that for a team, having a diversity of experience is optimal for creating new, high-impact ideas and having a diversity in expertise is the best for pumping out those ideas. The hackathon experience also showed me that generally, ideas that have more futuristic technology and grander goals (medicine as a whole is highly valued) tend to win. ARUW

ARUW was a thoroughly novel experience, even as a previous project manager. There was just so much going on and so many new things to quickly adapt to—more like working in a small company. However, the experience has been pretty eye-opening to me, and I think in terms of technical experience, this is the place where I’ve grown the most. First of all, I found out just how much math goes into robotics and how important differential equations, physics, and the internet are in trying to control where a robot points its turret. I was also introduced to the web development framework Django, which is somehow even harder to set up than Angular and runs on a new kind of paradigm that’s less component-based but is designed to work with databases in mind. While it’s had some bumps, the leaders have been very helpful and it’s been a fine experience overall.
